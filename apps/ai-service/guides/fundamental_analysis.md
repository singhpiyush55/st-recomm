# Fundamental Analysis Guide for Swing Trading (Indian Market / NSE)

This guide is injected as context into the LLM when it interprets fundamental ratios. The model should use these rules to evaluate a stock's financial health for swing trades lasting 2-6 weeks on the Indian market (NSE/BSE).

---

## Valuation Metrics

### P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
- Below 15: Potentially undervalued, but check if earnings are declining.
- 15-25: Fair value for a growth company. Acceptable for swing trades.
- Above 25: Only acceptable if revenue growth exceeds 20% YoY. Otherwise, overvalued and risky for swings.
- Negative P/E: Company is unprofitable. Avoid unless it's a high-growth tech name with improving margins.

### PEG Ratio (Price/Earnings-to-Growth)
- Below 1.0: Strongly undervalued relative to growth. This is the single best value signal for swing trades.
- 1.0-1.5: Fairly valued. Acceptable if other fundamentals are strong.
- Above 2.0: Overvalued relative to growth. The market is pricing in too much optimism.
- Key insight: PEG below 1 combined with accelerating earnings is a high-conviction setup.

---

## Profitability

### Return on Equity (ROE)
- Above 20%: Excellent. Company generates strong returns on shareholder capital.
- 15-20%: Good. Meets the threshold for quality.
- 10-15%: Mediocre. Acceptable only if improving quarter-over-quarter.
- Below 10%: Weak. Avoid unless the company is in a capital-intensive sector (Power, Infrastructure, PSU Banks) where lower ROE is normal.
- Sector adjustments: Indian PSU banks typically have ROE of 8-14% which is acceptable. Private banks should be above 14%. IT companies should be above 20%. FMCG companies often have ROE above 25% due to asset-light models.

### Return on Assets (ROA)
- Above 10%: Strong asset efficiency.
- 5-10%: Average.
- Below 5%: Poor unless the business is asset-heavy (manufacturing, airlines).
- Compare ROA across companies in the same sector, not across sectors.

---

## Growth

### Revenue Growth (Year-over-Year)
- Above 20%: High growth. Worth paying a premium P/E for this.
- 10-20%: Solid growth. Good for swing trades.
- 0-10%: Slow growth. Needs strong profitability to compensate.
- Negative: Contracting revenue. Avoid for swing trades unless there's a clear catalyst for reversal.

### EPS Growth (Year-over-Year)
- Accelerating EPS growth (this quarter's growth > last quarter's growth) is the strongest bullish signal.
- Positive but decelerating EPS growth is a warning sign — the stock may stall.
- Negative EPS growth combined with negative revenue growth is a strong avoid signal.
- Look for "earnings surprise" — stocks that beat estimates by more than 5% tend to trend higher for 3-4 weeks after earnings.

---

## Balance Sheet Health

### Debt-to-Equity Ratio
- Below 0.5: Very conservative balance sheet. Strong.
- 0.5-1.0: Manageable debt. Acceptable for most sectors.
- 1.0-2.0: Elevated leverage. Acceptable only for capital-intensive industries (Power, Infrastructure, Telecom, Metal).
- Above 2.0: High risk. Avoid for swing trades — leveraged companies are more volatile in downturns.
- Exception: Banking and NBFC companies naturally carry higher D/E ratios due to their business model. Use Capital Adequacy Ratio (CAR) and NPA ratios instead for Indian banks.

### Interest Coverage Ratio
- Above 10x: Very safe. Earnings easily cover interest payments.
- 5-10x: Adequate.
- Below 3x: Danger zone. Company may struggle to service debt if earnings decline.
- Below 1x: Company cannot cover interest payments. Strong avoid.

---

## Cash Flow

### Free Cash Flow (FCF)
- Positive and growing FCF is the most reliable indicator of financial health.
- FCF yield (FCF / market cap) above 5% is attractive.
- Negative FCF is acceptable only for early-stage growth companies investing heavily in expansion.
- Watch for the gap between net income and FCF. If net income is positive but FCF is negative, earnings quality is poor — the company may be using aggressive accounting.

---

## Red Flags to Always Mention
1. Revenue declining while debt is increasing
2. ROE above 30% driven by extreme leverage (high D/E) rather than operational efficiency
3. P/E expanding while EPS growth is decelerating
4. Free cash flow negative for more than 2 consecutive years in a mature company
5. Interest coverage below 3x combined with debt-to-equity above 1.5

---

## How to Structure Your Analysis
1. Start with the verdict: Strong, Medium, or Weak
2. Identify the 2-3 strongest fundamental positives
3. Identify the 1-2 biggest concerns
4. State whether the fundamentals support or contradict the technical setup
5. Keep the narrative to 3-4 sentences — concise and actionable
